**Abstact:** As the adoption of electric vehicles (EV) grows, the need for better planning of future charging infrastructure becomes increasingly critical. However, while prior research focuses on station placement and economic factors, fewer studies examine preferences on charging station venues at final destinations. This study examines these preferences among EV and non-EV users using a stated preference survey of 1,153 Indiana adults. Two sub-samples are considered: EV users (n = 177), who currently own an EV, and non-EV users (n = 976), who have never or previously owned one. EV users offer insights into real-world charging behavior, while non-EV users represent future potential adopters, making their preferences essential for infrastructure planning.  This study evaluates participants’ perceived charging frequency if public charging stations were available across seven different venue types—office, retail, medical, educational, transit, hotel, and restaurant— assessing factors that influence their stated preferences using a multivariate ordered probit model.   For EV users, significant factors included employment status, household non-EV ownership, and travel frequency. Frequent travelers were less likely to charge at educational, retail, and transit locations, while those with more non-EVs in their household were less likely to charge at retail and medical venues. For non-EV users, significant factors included age, home type, incentive awareness, travel frequency, and urbanity. Older adults were less likely to charge in these venues, particularly offices, while those in urban areas were more likely to charge at educational and office locations.  By identifying factors influencing charging preferences, this study informs future charging infrastructure investment and policy planning.
